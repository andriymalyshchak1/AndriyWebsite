How I think about product intuition

Andriy

There's a version of product work that looks like spreadsheets, frameworks, and prioritization matrices. That version is real — but it's not where the most important decisions actually happen. The ones that matter tend to arrive quietly, in the form of a feeling that something is off.

Most people who build products long enough develop a kind of internal signal — a pull toward or away from a direction that's hard to articulate in a planning doc. Call it taste, call it intuition, call it pattern recognition. Whatever it is, it's the thing that separates the people who make things that resonate from the people who make things that are merely correct.

The best product decisions I've made didn't come from frameworks. They came from noticing — and having the courage to act on what I noticed.

Why this matters

We're in a moment where AI can generate options endlessly. Any product direction you're considering, a model can now produce fifty variants of in minutes. That abundance makes the framework layer cheaper, not more valuable. What it can't replicate is the judgment about which of those fifty actually matters — and why.

Product intuition is the last thing to be abstracted. It lives in the details you notice, the customers you've watched struggle, the moments where something shipped and felt wrong even though it tested well. None of that lives in a model. It lives in you.

What I've learned

The most useful thing I've done to build better intuition is stay close to things that aren't working. Not to fix them immediately, but to sit with the wrongness long enough to understand it. There's a habit most product people develop of immediately pattern-matching a problem to a solution. Breaking that habit — even briefly — is where the real insight tends to live.

The other thing: write down what you expected to happen before you ship something. Not a PRD, not a spec — just a sentence. "I think users will do X." Then go look at what they actually did. The gap between those two things is the most useful signal I've found. Do it enough times and you stop predicting wrong in the same ways. That's intuition.
